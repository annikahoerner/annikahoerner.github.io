# first-hand experience with uncertainty
Over the past two years working socio-spatial household survey data from across Sub-Saharan Africa, I have become intimately familiar with the endless uncertainty that emerges when you start to make analytical decisions. 

Particularly as we begin to develop statistical regression models to predict outcomes such as benefit, wealth, and well-being, it becomes a big decision to determine what factors to include within the model. More than this decision, however, is how we need to work with the limits of the data that we already have. For the research I was involved in, a separate organization conducted the surveys and data collection and then passed the data to us researchers, so we did the scientific process somewhat backwards. We looked at the variables and data that we had and started to see what stories could emerge from that existing data pool. 

This process has a number of issues, but is particularly challenging for statistical tests, where we can only control with the few variables that we had. It is always difficult to model/predict social data, but this additional lack of discretion and control over what variables we can work with pushes the uncertainty of the final result into a different realm. 

Within the framework of Longley et al's conceptual framework of uncertainty, the filters that are dissociating the statistical tests from the real world are many and layered -- especially because there is such a nonlinear path from study conception to design to analysis; each of these steps were conducted in disjointed and backwards steps.
