# open source GIS and reproducibility

### open source gis is helpful
Open soure GIS refers to the movement that wants to make GIS software more accessible through reducing the barriers to accessing and understanding the osftware. As such, it's a helpful step towards amending issues of reproducibility in geography through removing barriers of access to academic and data-driven software. For reproducibility in particular — which is concerned with the ability to return similar and like results answering a given question — this increases the ability for checks and balances across the scientific community. Open source software allows researchers/citizens/the audience of scientific publications to follow the methodology of an article and apply that to their own dataset. GIS software has been historically proprietary and gate kept by corporations, which limits who is able to participate in the act of research and knowledge production. Open source GIS therefore not only aids in the reproducibility crisis in geography, but in the crisis of homogeneity in terms of the researchers themselves. 

### open source **&** free is better
While open source allows users to understand the inner workings of the software that they interface with, it does not always mean that the software is free. Open source software that adopts the mindset of free software - a decentralized model where anyone can assist in its development - is helpful for what Rey et al. describe as the "bazaar" organization of software development, where code is produced in more of a *marketplace* than decided upon and distributed in a *top-down* manner. As Rey et al. argue, it represents a philsophy that extends beyond the *accessibility* of software and information and to a reorganization of the way that we think about knowledge and its production. This is more than "open source GIS" -- it's about open source community and a social network comprised of those with open minds. 

### open source software cannot stand alone
Along these lines of an open community & mind -- ultimately the software and access to the code still leaves one critical aspect of reproducibility up to the researcher: **inferential reproducibility** *(via [NASEM](https://nap.nationalacademies.org/catalog/25303/reproducibility-and-replicability-in-science))*

We cannot rely on software itself to amend issues of transparency, replicability, and justice within academia. There remains the critical component of human interpretation, which is pretty difficult to make open source. While we can continue to report degrees of confidence and the data outputs, interpretation and discretion remains — which is a particular concern as funding for research continues to be a political issue. While we can promote open source software as an expanding tool that reduces barriers to access, it remains critical to back the software up with education and the scaffolding of thorough scientific processes.
